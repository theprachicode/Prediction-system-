Understanding DPN

Causes, Risk Factors, and Clinical Context

Detailed Explanation

Diabetic Peripheral Neuropathy (DPN) is a progressive condition characterized by nerve damage resulting from chronic hyperglycemia. The structural damage primarily affects the peripheral nervous system, particularly the distal sensory and motor nerves of the lower extremities.

Over time, prolonged exposure to high blood glucose levels damages the delicate capillary blood vessels (vasa nervorum) that supply the nerves, leading to ischemia and neuronal dysfunction.

Primary Causes

  • Metabolic factors: High blood glucose, long duration of diabetes, abnormal blood fat levels, and possibly low levels of insulin.
  • Neurovascular factors: Damage to the blood vessels that carry oxygen and nutrients to nerves.
  • Autoimmune factors: Inflammation that causes the immune system to attack nerves.

Risk Factors

  • Poor glycemic control: High HbA1c levels directly correlate with nerve damage.
  • Diabetes duration: The risk increases the longer you have diabetes.
  • Kidney disease: Toxins can accumulate and damage nerves.
  • BMI/Obesity: A body mass index greater than 25 increases risk.
  • Smoking & Alcohol: Narrows and hardens arteries, reducing blood flow to legs.
